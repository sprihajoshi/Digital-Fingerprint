Climate Systems

The global climate system is driven by interactions between five major components: the atmosphere, hydrosphere, cryosphere, biosphere, and lithosphere. Energy from the sun flows through each of these, driving atmospheric circulation, ocean currents, and the water cycle.

Greenhouse gases

Carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), and water vapour act as greenhouse gases, trapping outgoing infrared radiation and raising surface temperatures — a process known as the greenhouse effect. Human activity has increased atmospheric CO₂ concentrations from roughly 280 ppm pre-industrial to over 420 ppm today.

Feedback mechanisms

The climate system contains several reinforcing and dampening feedback loops:

  • Ice-albedo feedback — as ice melts, darker ocean or land surface is exposed, absorbing more solar energy and accelerating warming.
  • Water vapour feedback — warmer air holds more water vapour, itself a greenhouse gas, amplifying initial temperature increases.
  • Cloud feedback — changes in cloud cover can either amplify or dampen warming depending on cloud type and altitude.

Energy balance

Earth's energy balance determines long-term temperature. When incoming solar energy exceeds outgoing infrared radiation, the planet warms. Current greenhouse gas concentrations create a radiative forcing of approximately 3 W/m², pushing the system out of equilibrium.

Key uncertainties

Regional climate projections remain uncertain due to the non-linear nature of feedback mechanisms and limitations in modelling cloud behaviour, ocean heat uptake, and ice sheet dynamics at fine spatial scales.
