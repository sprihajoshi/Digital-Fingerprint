Language Models

Large language models (LLMs) are neural networks trained on vast text corpora to learn statistical associations between tokens. They have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across a wide range of language tasks, from summarisation and translation to code generation and reasoning.

Training objective

Most modern LLMs are trained using self-supervised objectives — typically next-token prediction, where the model learns to predict the next word in a sequence given all preceding words. This simple objective, applied at massive scale, produces models that generalise broadly across tasks without task-specific supervision.

Transformer architecture

The transformer architecture underpins most modern LLMs:

  • Attention mechanisms — allow the model to relate any token to any other token in the context window, regardless of distance.
  • Stacked layers — each layer captures increasingly abstract linguistic representations, from syntax to semantics to world knowledge.
  • Scale — model capability grows with the number of parameters, training tokens, and compute, following empirical scaling laws.

Emergent capabilities

At sufficient scale, LLMs exhibit capabilities that were not explicitly trained for — including multi-step reasoning, analogical thinking, and in-context learning. These emergent behaviours are not fully understood and are an active area of research.

Energy and compute

Training large models requires substantial energy and specialised hardware. A single large training run can consume megawatt-hours of electricity, raising questions about the environmental footprint of scaling and the concentration of AI development among well-resourced organisations.
