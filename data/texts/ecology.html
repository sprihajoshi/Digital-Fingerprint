Ecosystem Dynamics

Ecosystems are characterised by flows of energy and matter through food webs, cycling nutrients between producers, consumers, and decomposers across multiple trophic levels. These flows are governed by thermodynamic constraints — energy is lost at each trophic transfer, limiting the length of food chains.

Trophic structure

Energy enters most ecosystems through photosynthesis, where producers convert solar energy into biomass. Consumers obtain energy by feeding on producers or other consumers, while decomposers break down dead organic matter and return nutrients to the soil:

  • Producers — plants, algae, and cyanobacteria that fix solar energy into organic compounds.
  • Consumers — herbivores, carnivores, and omnivores that obtain energy by feeding on other organisms.
  • Decomposers — fungi and bacteria that mineralise organic matter, making nutrients available for uptake by producers.

Keystone species and trophic cascades

Keystone species exert disproportionate influence on ecosystem structure relative to their biomass. The removal of a keystone predator can trigger trophic cascades — a chain of indirect effects that restructures community composition and alters ecosystem function. The reintroduction of wolves to Yellowstone is a well-documented example.

Disturbance regimes

Disturbance — fire, flood, drought, storm — is not simply destructive. Many ecosystems depend on periodic disturbance to maintain biodiversity and prevent competitive exclusion, where dominant species eliminate others. The intermediate disturbance hypothesis predicts peak diversity at moderate disturbance frequency.

Temperature and ecosystem function

Temperature mediates the rate of nearly all biological processes — photosynthesis, respiration, decomposition, and nutrient cycling. Climate warming is shifting species ranges, altering phenological timing, and disrupting the synchrony between interacting species, with cascading effects on ecosystem function.
